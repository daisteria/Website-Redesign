Process Document

Overview

This is a documented process of the creation and production of this website.

Please note that the number four is unviewable in this font family. They appear like 4 in some sections. Other unsupported characters that I found was substituted to the best of my ability, with the exception of the forward slash and hashtag, which I could not substitute. I was unable to debug these issues with the downloaded CC open licensed font, so I ask for your consideration in overlooking this issue. The overall site is still legible, but might be confusing when the unsupported character is displayed.

Information Architecture

Original Information Architecture from the Car Wikipedia page.

The original information architecture derived from Wikipedia’s car page contained a total of 21 subsections, most of which did not contain any child subsections. Some of these categories could be compiled into a single directory since their information is similar. For instance, the ‘User Interface’ and ‘Electronics and Interior’ sections both contain information that could fall under a single ‘Car interior’ category, and the ‘Environmental Issues’ and ‘Social Issues’ both denote issues about cars. Some categories, like the subsections under ‘Emerging Car Technologies’, contained information that was already repeated in the long ‘History’ section, and so these subdirectories were removed in the revised information architecture for redundancy.

Revised Information Architecture.

Upon analyzing the original subsections, all categories could be reorganized into relevant and broad topics. As such, the global structural navigation headers now consist of topics that contain information about cars, the history, the specifications, considerations, and further external information. This pertains to Brown’s principle of front doors, which assumes that at least half of the website’s visitors will come to the site through some other subpage. Having a list of global structural navigation headers that encompass all the content in the website at the top of the pages for global access will allow such users to navigate through the site more efficiently. The organization of these new headers also follow the principle of focused navigation, where a site’s most crucial topic navigation features separate and distinct information.

To add, the categories have been labelled in accordance to its navigation status for implementation. The global structural headers are to be added at the top as aforementioned, and the utility is to be implemented in the footer of the website. The subsections of these categories are found in their respective pages, which were moved to categories that their content best fit. This follows Brown’s principle of objects, which is the organization of similar content, as it takes similar sections from the original information architecture and compiles it in a single topic in the revised version.

This revised information architecture in general satisfies Brown’s principle of growth, which is the assumption that a website’s current content is just a fraction of the content that it will have in the future. In other words, a site should organize their content in such a way that it will be easier to add information after initial implementation. The revised global navigation categories are designed to be broad enough to encompass all information about cars. This also ensures that any future added content can likely fall under one of the main global topics. If this is not the case, then a new global directory can be added easily.

The following sketches and calculations are my personal documentation based on the University of Winnipeg’s website on the left. The images and XD files are not necessarily wireframes, but rather a general outline of the sections found on the university website’s landing page and a few pages that I chose to use to model my replicate website on. Prototype and Building

Following the sketches and wireframe estimations, the final prototype was created.

Upon inspecting the UWinnipeg website, I found that the font family used was a free licensed Adobe font called ‘proxima–nova’. As such, I have downloaded and imported this font into the fonts folder at “/a1/fonts/Demo_Fonts/”. In addition, all webpage titles follow the general format of “current–page, ../, ../ ...”, and so this is reflected in each page’s title.

Overall, the pages are a 1:1 replica to the original website, with the differences primarily shown in content and perhaps a few pixels off of the original layout due to human error and eye estimation （I used a physical ruler on my screen and sometimes my fingernail while alt tabbing between the UWinnipeg and my own site）.

Note that most of the links found on the landing page are fully functional; i.e. the ‘Featured News’ and ‘Upcoming Car Events’ links all lead to their respective references, and all references to these are found in the Media section below for added clarity. In the header, the ‘Wiki’ link directs to the Car Wikipedia page. In the global navigation under, the logo leads to the home page like the UWinnipeg site, and the ‘Exterior’, ‘Lighting’, ‘Weight’, and ‘Body Style’ links all direct to the Exterior inner page as per the Project Criteria. The ‘Process’ tab leads to this page. In the footer, the ‘Exteriors’ link under Information leads to the Exterior page. The ‘Original Website’ in the section after this footer navigation leads to the Wiki page.

Landing Page: Header
Finished header with borders for visualization.

Creating the header was simple: creating a container that spans the width of the whole screen, and then aligning a child container inside that will contain the links and search icon. The links on the left are justified to the start, and the right links and search are justified to the end. I also added the animation that a faint underline appears when each link is hovered over.

Landing Page: Global Header Navigation
Finished global navigation. Not hovered.

This section sits on top of the spotlight, so the position must be absolute and it must have a z index higher than the spotlight for stacking layers. Like the header, it contains a left and right container in the child container of the parent nav section. At its current state, the section background is transparent with a border at the bottom.

Note that at this point, the revised IA category ‘System Interface’ is revised to ‘Specifications’ for simplicity and shortening. It still categorizes the original revised subdirectories.

Finished global navigation. Hovered with finished overlay.

Courtesy of this, the animation of the bottom border expanding from the middle when a link is hovered over is replicated on the site. In addition, the overlay container follows the absolute positioning of the nav section over the spotlight section. The overlay consists of four columns, all with max widths as estimated guesses.

The button in the first column of the overlay also has an animation courtesy of this. The links in the other columns also have animations according to the UWinnipeg website. Namely, upon hover, the second and third column links translate to the right and turn red, and the related links column links turn red only.

Landing Page: Spotlight
Finished spotlight.

The spotlight contains a background video, which was edited; the process can be found in the Media section. The h2 title and subtext have a slight textshadow to avoid bad contrasting against the video, maintaining the WCAG guidelines. The play and pause button follows the original website, which I found to be slightly harder about building this website, since it required a bit of research and recollection of my Java knowledge. Ultimately, the script and reference can be found before the end tag of the body section. In addition, the chevron arrow pointing down scrolls the viewport to the intro, or next, section.

Finished intro.

Finished Events section.

The events section contains three columns in the child container, and each column is an inline flex container justified at the top. Each column contains a red square that contains the date that mimics the UWinnipeg site to the best of my ability, given that the font sometimes does not support heavily bolded text nor the number four. It also makes use of the ‘More news’ button class.

Landing Page: Footer Navigation
Finished Footer first section part.

This section contains two blocks: one with four columns, and the other with a simple text. The columns are fit and padded to best mimic the original website footer, and also have the same animations such as the social links, the Donate button, and hovering over the utility. Only the ‘Exterior’ link directs to another page.

At this point, the revised IA labels no longer truly apply; for instance, the Interior and Exterior associative categories of the Global Structural ‘Specifications’ are now in Utility too. However, since it was initially assigned as associative labels at the start of the website, i.e. in the global navigation bar overlay, these labels still maintain since the site’s visitors will see these associative links first before they see it in the footer. To add, these associative links were added alongside the utility labels merely for design demonstration purposes, or to fill up the footer with content.

Landing Page: Bottom Footer
Finished Footer bottom section.

This follows the header layout, except without the use of the right container. The links are not functional, but still show the animation that is used in the UWinnipeg site.

Inner Page: Exterior
Finished and reevaluated inner page. Chose Exterior as the content to show depth and actual text content rather than a page with just associative links.

At this point, I reevaluated the website page that this would replicate, which was originally the Research webpage. There were many unnecessary items that weren't suitable for the content, i.e. an events section on the left, and highlights at the bottom. So, instead, I chose this page instead. It still contains the same layout, meaning that the wireframes are still applicable to the inner page, but this new page no longer keeps the events and highlights section. This way, the content is better suited to fill the page while maintaining the design.

As explained in the Wireframes section, inner pages typically use two columns. This is demonstrated in the image, where the contents are divided by a left column and a bigger sized right column. What was not mentioned in the Wireframes section, and something that I realized upon starting to build the inner page, is that the child containers of every section is smaller in width than the home page. In CSS, the .container width does not apply to the inner pages. To fix this, I set the header, nav, and footer section to adjust to a new .others-container, which sets the appropriate width that replicates the UWinnipeg site.

Another format not accounted for in the Wireframes section is that the navigation section is a permanent white background, and has a box shadow. Since this navigation style is applied globally to all pages, I decided to give the nav tag in inner pages a class. This class, .nav-others, is formatted to override the existing nav presets and set the background colour to white, set a box shadow akin to the UWinnipeg site, and keep the logo on its black version.

Logo by Shmector.com at shmector.com. The original files included can be found in “a1/docs/logo”. Edited in Adobe Illustrator to fit the prototype and can be found at “/a1/docs/logo/ car–logo–edited.ai”. The white version is a copy of this edit, with ‘style: “fill:white;”’ at the end of the path.

Video

As mentioned in Visual Studies, the car playlist was a collection of car cinematics that I felt encapsulated the aesthetic of displaying a car's sleek and smooth manufacturing and finish. I was heavily leaning towards this video, because I wanted a video that showed multiple car types rather than just one, since this site is based on the automobile concept and not a specific vehicle brand type. After hours, I find this video. This shows eight different car types, and satisfies my search for a cinematic type of showcasing these automobiles.

However, there are many shots that do not feature a car; i.e. the helicopter showcase shots. Thus, I edited the video on Adobe Premiere Pro 2023 to splice those shots out. Upon adding it to my home page site, I realize that the video is somewhat too bright. This affects WCAG, since the contrast between the spotlight and nav text will be too low for accessibility standards. So, I re–edited the video and added a black rectangle over the whole clip at 75 percent opacity. This way, the spotlight is dim, passing WCAG contrasting standards, and primarily giving focus to the text that overlays.
